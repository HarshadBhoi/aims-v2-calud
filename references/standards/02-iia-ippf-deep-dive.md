# IIA Global Internal Audit Standards (GIAS 2024) — Deep Dive Study

## Formerly: International Professional Practices Framework (IPPF)
### Institute of Internal Auditors

> **IMPORTANT**: The IIA replaced the 2017 IPPF with the **Global Internal Audit Standards (GIAS)** on **January 9, 2024**. GIAS became **effective January 9, 2025**. The structure is fundamentally different from prior IPPF — this document reflects the current GIAS 2024.

## 2. GIAS 2024 Fundamental Structure

The 2017 IPPF had a layered structure: Mission + Core Principles + Definition + Code of Ethics + Standards (Attribute 1000s, Performance 2000s) + Implementation Guidance + Supplemental Guidance.

**GIAS 2024 collapsed this into a single integrated document** organized into:
- **5 Domains**
- **15 Principles**
- **52 Standards**

### The 5 Domains

| Domain | Title | Principles | Focus |
|--------|-------|------------|-------|
| **I** | Purpose of Internal Auditing | (Standalone purpose statement) | Foundational value statement |
| **II** | Ethics and Professionalism | Principles 1-5 | Who auditors must BE |
| **III** | Governing the Internal Audit Function | Principles 6-8 | Board-level requirements |
| **IV** | Managing the Internal Audit Function | Principles 9-12 | CAE-level requirements |
| **V** | Performing Internal Audit Services | Principles 13-15 | Engagement execution |

### The 15 Principles

**Domain II — Ethics and Professionalism**
1. Demonstrate Integrity
2. Maintain Objectivity
3. Demonstrate Competency
4. Exercise Due Professional Care
5. Maintain Confidentiality

**Domain III — Governing the Internal Audit Function**
6. Authorized by the Board
7. Positioned Independently
8. Overseen by the Board

**Domain IV — Managing the Internal Audit Function**
9. Plan Strategically
10. Manage Resources
11. Communicate Effectively
12. Enhance Quality

**Domain V — Performing Internal Audit Services**
13. Plan Engagements Effectively
14. Conduct Engagement Work
15. Communicate Engagement Results and Monitor Action Plans

### Four Required Elements per Standard (Major Operational Change)

Each standard in GIAS 2024 has a **consistent 4-part structure**:

1. **Requirements** — mandatory "must" statements
2. **Considerations for Implementation** — practical "how to" guidance (replaces former Implementation Guidance)
3. **Examples of Evidence of Conformance** — what auditors/assessors look for (**NEW**)
4. **Topical Requirements** (where applicable) — specific topic overlays (first topical: cybersecurity)

### Essential Conditions (Domain III)
The conditions that must exist for internal audit to conform — primarily in Domain III:
- Board authorization
- Independent positioning
- Board oversight

### Standards Numbering
**Old 2017**: 1000-1322 (Attribute), 2000-2600 (Performance)
**New GIAS**: Standards numbered as `Domain.Principle.Sequence` — e.g., Standard 9.1, Standard 13.3, Standard 14.2

## 4. Domain II — Ethics and Professionalism (Principles 1-5)

### Principle 1: Demonstrate Integrity
- Honest, transparent, accountable behavior
- Compliance with laws, regulations, and ethical standards

### Principle 2: Maintain Objectivity
- Unbiased mental attitude
- No conflicts of interest
- Professional skepticism

### Principle 3: Demonstrate Competency
- Required knowledge, skills, and experience
- **Standard 3.1 CPD requirement**: 40 hours per year for CIA (maintained from prior IPPF)
- Collective competence across audit team

### Principle 4: Exercise Due Professional Care
- Reasonable care and diligence
- Critical evaluation of evidence
- Appropriate audit procedures

### Principle 5: Maintain Confidentiality
- Protect information
- Use only for authorized purposes
- Appropriate disclosures when required by law

**Software implications**: Code of ethics module, independence declarations, CPD tracking, conflict of interest register

---

## 5. Domain III — Governing the Internal Audit Function (Principles 6-8)

### Principle 6: Authorized by the Board
- **Audit Charter** formally approved by Board
- Defines: purpose, authority, responsibility, scope of services

**Required Charter Contents (Standards 6.1-6.2)**:
1. Purpose, authority, responsibility
2. Reporting lines (functional to Board, administrative to management)
3. Nature of services (assurance and consulting)
4. Board oversight responsibilities (Principle 8)
5. Unrestricted access to records, personnel, physical properties
6. Independence and objectivity statements
7. Commitment to conform with GIAS
8. QAIP commitment
9. Scope of internal audit activities
10. Approval and review cycle (annual review required)

### Principle 7: Positioned Independently
- CAE reports **functionally to the Board** (audit committee)
- Administrative reporting to management acceptable (not to CFO per best practice)
- Board must protect CAE from interference
- Unrestricted Board access

### Principle 8: Overseen by the Board
- Board must actively oversee internal audit
- Approves charter, strategy, annual plan, budget
- Evaluates CAE performance
- Reviews QAIP results
- Executive sessions with CAE (no management present)

**Software implications**: Audit Charter template, approval workflow, version tracking, Board reporting dashboard, CAE performance evaluation tool

## 6. Domain IV — Managing the Internal Audit Function (Principles 9-12)

### Principle 9: Plan Strategically (NEW emphasis)
**Standard 9.1 explicitly requires a written Internal Audit Strategy** (new elevation vs 2017)
- Strategy aligns with organization's strategic objectives
- Multi-year horizon
- Updated periodically

**Risk-based audit planning**:
- Audit universe development
- Risk factors (inherent, residual, strategic, operational, financial, compliance, fraud, cybersecurity, ESG)
- Risk ranking methodologies
- Multi-year rolling plans (3-year typical for private, 5-year for government)
- Board approval of annual plan

### Principle 10: Manage Resources
- Adequate financial, human, and technology resources
- Skills matrix across team
- Training and development plan
- Technology enablement (audit management systems, data analytics)

### Principle 11: Communicate Effectively
- Regular communication with Board and management
- Quality, accurate, timely communications
- Escalation protocols for disagreements

### Principle 12: Enhance Quality (QAIP)
- Quality Assurance and Improvement Program
- Internal assessments (ongoing + periodic)
- External assessments (minimum every 5 years)

**QAIP Deep Dive**:

| Assessment Type | Frequency | Method |
|-----------------|-----------|--------|
| Ongoing monitoring | Continuous | Supervision, file reviews, surveys |
| Periodic internal self-assessment | Every 3-5 years | Structured conformance review |
| External assessment — Full EQA | Every 5 years | Qualified external assessor |
| External assessment — SAIV (alternative) | Every 5 years | Internal self-assessment + independent validator |

**Conformance Ratings**:
| Rating | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **Generally Conforms** | Substantial conformance; few opportunities |
| **Partially Conforms** | Deficiencies affect conformance but not serious |
| **Does Not Conform** | Deficiencies so significant they impair ability to discharge responsibilities |

**Assessor Qualifications**: CIA certification + competence in QA + independence
**CQA (Certified Quality Assessor)**: IIA training/credential for QA assessors (strongly preferred)

**Software implications**: Strategy document management, audit universe, risk scoring engine, resource/capacity tracker, QAIP checklist, conformance dashboard

## 7. Domain V — Performing Internal Audit Services (Principles 13-15)

### Principle 13: Plan Engagements Effectively
- Define objectives, scope, methodology
- Risk assessment per engagement
- Resource assignment
- Communication with engagement client
- Written engagement plan

### Principle 14: Conduct Engagement Work
- Obtain sufficient, reliable, relevant evidence
- Document information (workpapers)
- Analyze and evaluate evidence
- **Root cause analysis** (explicit emphasis)
- Supervision and review

### Principle 15: Communicate Engagement Results and Monitor Action Plans
- Accurate, objective, clear, concise, constructive, timely, complete communications
- Engagement report with:
  - Criteria (what should be)
  - Condition (what is)
  - Cause (root cause)
  - Consequence/Effect (impact)
  - Recommendation
- Management responses and action plans
- Follow-up and monitoring
- Risk acceptance escalation to Board (when management accepts risk without action)

### Finding Elements (Consistent across Domain V)
Each finding must include:
| Element | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Criteria** | What should be | "Policy requires competitive bid for purchases > $50K" |
| **Condition** | What is | "4 of 15 purchases over $50K were not competitively bid" |
| **Cause** | Why the gap exists | "Manager unaware of threshold; no training provided" |
| **Consequence/Effect** | Impact | "~$200K annual overage; compliance risk" |
| **Recommendation** | How to fix | "Implement mandatory training + automated alerts" |

**Ratings**: Critical, Major, Minor (or similar)

**Software implications**: Engagement planning form, evidence management, workpaper repository, supervision workflow, finding form with 5 elements, CAP tracking, risk acceptance register

## 8. Engagement Types: Assurance vs. Consulting

| Aspect | Assurance | Consulting |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| **Purpose** | Independent opinion on effectiveness | Advice and recommendations |
| **Scope** | Fixed; predefined by plan | Flexible; client-driven |
| **Client** | Organization/Board | Operational management |
| **Reporting** | Formal audit report | Less formal advisory |
| **Independence** | Must be high | Can be collaborative |
| **Opinion** | Yes (on control effectiveness) | No (recommendations only) |
| **Examples** | Operational, compliance, financial, IT security audits | Process improvement, control design, technology guidance, post-implementation reviews |

## 9. Three Lines Model (July 2020 Update)

In July 2020, IIA replaced "Three Lines of Defense" with the **Three Lines Model** — a philosophical shift from defensive to value-creation orientation.

### Key Changes 2013 → 2020

| 2013 "Three Lines of Defense" | 2020 "Three Lines Model" |
|-------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Defensive metaphor | Value-creation orientation |
| Rigid line separation | Principles-based, collaborative |
| Narrow risk focus | Governance + risk + value |
| Internal audit as last defense | Internal audit as independent assurance |

### Six Principles of the 2020 Model
1. Governance
2. Governing body roles
3. Management and first & second line roles
4. Third line roles (internal audit)
5. Third line independence
6. Creating and protecting value

### Roles

| Role | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Governing Body** (Board) | Accountability to stakeholders, oversight |
| **Management — 1st Line** | Owns and manages risk directly; provides products/services |
| **Management — 2nd Line** | Expertise, support, monitoring, challenge (Risk, Compliance, Quality, Legal, Finance controllership) |
| **Internal Audit — 3rd Line** | Independent, objective assurance and advice |
| **External Assurance Providers** (outside the model) | External auditors, regulators, other external bodies |

## 10. Active IIA Certifications (2024-2025)

### CIA (Certified Internal Auditor) — Flagship Credential

**3-part exam** (restructured from 4 parts in 2019):

**Part 1 — Essentials of Internal Auditing** (125 questions, 2.5 hours)
- Foundations of IA (15%)
- Independence & Objectivity (15%)
- Proficiency & Due Professional Care (18%)
- QAIP (7%)
- Governance/Risk/Control (35%)
- Fraud Risks (10%)

**Part 2 — Practice of Internal Auditing** (100 questions, 2 hours)
- Managing IA Activity (20%)
- Planning the Engagement (20%)
- Performing the Engagement (40%)
- Communicating Results & Monitoring Progress (20%)

**Part 3 — Business Knowledge for Internal Auditing** (100 questions, 2 hours)
- Business Acumen (35%)
- Information Security (25%)
- Information Technology (20%)
- Financial Management (20%)

**Eligibility**: Bachelor's + 24 months IA experience (or Master's + 12 months, or 5 years without degree per 2024 rules)
**Passing Score**: 600 on 250-750 scaled scoring (~70-75% raw)

### CRMA (Certification in Risk Management Assurance)
- Standalone 2-hour, 100-question exam (CIA no longer prerequisite as of 2020)
- Domains: Organizational Governance Related to Risk Management (20%), Principles of Risk Management (25%), Assurance Role (30%), Consulting Role (25%)

### Internal Audit Practitioner
- Entry-level credential (introduced ~2020)
- For junior auditors or non-IA professionals interested in the field

### CIA Challenge Exam
- For CPAs and other qualified professionals — expedited CIA pathway

### DISCONTINUED Certifications (2023 sunset)
| Certification | Status |
|---------------|--------|
| **CGAP** (Certified Government Auditing Professional) | Discontinued Dec 31, 2023 |
| **CFSA** (Certified Financial Services Auditor) | Discontinued 2023 |
| **CCSA** (Certification in Control Self-Assessment) | Discontinued 2023 |
| **QIAL** (Qualification in Internal Audit Leadership) | Discontinued 2021 |

## 12. Implementation Standards Clarification

**Important correction**: The 2017 IPPF used **`.A1/.A2` suffixes for Assurance** and **`.C1/.C2` suffixes for Consulting** implementation standards appended to the 2000-series (NOT a separate 3000-3999 series as sometimes miscited).

Historical examples:
- **2130.A1**: Internal audit activity must evaluate governance, risk management, controls (assurance)
- **2130.C1**: During consulting, auditors address controls consistent with engagement objectives
- **2201.A1**: When planning for parties outside organization, written understanding required
- **2440.A2**: Before releasing results outside organization, CAE must assess risk and consult legal

In GIAS 2024, this distinction is restructured into each standard's **Requirements** subsection with explicit applicability statements (assurance-only, consulting-only, or both).

## 13. Agile Auditing (Emerging Practice)

### IIA Guidance
- Practice Guide: "Agile Auditing" (circa 2021)
- GIAS 2024 principles-based structure accommodates agile methodology

### Core Agile Audit Concepts
- **Sprints**: 2-4 week cycles
- **Backlog**: Prioritized audit procedures
- **Stand-ups**: Daily team syncs
- **Retrospectives**: Continuous improvement
- **MVP (Minimum Viable Product)**: Incremental reporting
- **Client collaboration**: Iterative involvement

### Benefits
- Faster time-to-insight
- Incremental value delivery
- Better stakeholder alignment
- Reduced "big bang" failures

### Challenges
- Documentation rigor (must still meet GIAS evidence requirements)
- Scope creep risk
- Resource predictability
- Training curve

### Hybrid Approach
Many functions use agile for IT, advisory, and cybersecurity engagements; traditional for financial/compliance audits.

## 14. Fraud and Internal Audit

### GIAS 2024 Fraud Requirements
- **Principle 3** (Competency) — sufficient knowledge to evaluate fraud risk
- **Principle 4** (Due Professional Care) — alertness to fraud indicators
- **Standard 13.x** — engagement planning considers fraud risks
- **Standard 14.x** — procedures address identified fraud risks

### Fraud Risk Assessment Components
1. Identify fraud risk factors (Fraud Triangle: pressure/opportunity/rationalization)
2. Map to fraud schemes (ACFE taxonomy: asset misappropriation, corruption, financial statement fraud)
3. Assess likelihood and impact
4. Evaluate anti-fraud controls
5. Identify residual fraud risks requiring audit coverage

### Red Flag Categories
- **Behavioral**: Lifestyle changes, refusal to take vacation, control issues
- **Financial**: Unusual fluctuations, round-number entries, missing documentation
- **Process**: Control overrides, segregation of duties breakdowns
- **Data analytics**: Benford's Law violations, duplicate payments, ghost employees

### Forensic Audit Considerations
Internal audit typically **identifies and refers**, not conducts forensic investigations:
- Chain of custody protocols
- Legal privilege (attorney-client, work product)
- Coordination with Legal, HR, Compliance, external forensic accountants
- Evidence preservation (do not confront, document contemporaneously)

## 15. Data Analytics & Continuous Auditing

### Continuous Monitoring vs Continuous Auditing

| Dimension | Continuous Monitoring | Continuous Auditing |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Owner | Management (2nd line) | Internal Audit (3rd line) |
| Purpose | Operational/compliance awareness | Independent assurance |
| Frequency | Real-time / high frequency | Near real-time periodic |
| Scope | Management-defined | Risk-based audit plan |
| Reporting | To management | To Board + management |
| Independence | Not required | Required |

### Data Analytics Maturity Levels
1. **Ad hoc** — manual data requests, Excel analysis
2. **Repeatable** — standard analytics per engagement
3. **Defined** — analytics library, team sharing
4. **Managed** — analytics-first planning, automated pulls
5. **Optimized** — continuous auditing, ML/AI integration

### Common Analytics Techniques
- 100% population testing (replaces sampling for some tests)
- Benford's Law (fraud detection)
- Duplicate detection
- Gap analysis, stratification, aging
- Trend analysis, correlation, regression
- Anomaly detection (ML-based)

### RPA in Audit
- **Audit OF RPA bots**: Bot identity, SoD, change management
- **RPA FOR audit**: Automate data pulls, sample selection, report generation
- Key risks: bot sprawl, credential management, audit trail integrity

## 16. Board / Audit Committee Reporting

### Required Board Communications (Principle 8, 11)

**Each Audit Committee Meeting (quarterly)**:
- Plan execution status
- Summary of completed engagements
- Emerging risks
- Resource/budget status
- Open management actions status
- Significant issues

**Annually**:
- Charter review
- Annual plan approval
- Internal Audit Strategy update
- QAIP internal results
- CAE independence attestation
- Resource adequacy statement
- Overall opinion (if issued)

**Every 5 Years**:
- External QA results

**Ad Hoc / Escalation**:
- Material fraud
- Significant control failures
- Management override
- Scope impairment
- CAE departure/appointment

### Escalation Protocols
- **Direct access** to Audit Committee chair (functional reporting)
- **Executive sessions** with Board (no management present) — typically quarterly
- **Whistleblower/ethics** matters
- **Disagreements**: If CAE disagrees with management accepting unacceptable risk, escalate to Board

## 18. GIAS 2024 vs. GAGAS — Key Differences

| Aspect | GIAS 2024 (IIA) | GAGAS (Yellow Book) |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Scope** | All sectors (private, public, non-profit) | Government only |
| **Authority** | Professional framework (voluntary) | Federal requirement for govt audits |
| **Independence** | Professional standards (Principles 6-8) | Very strict; absolute requirement |
| **Cooling-Off** | 12 months typical | 12-24 months (stricter) |
| **CPE** | 40 hrs/yr (CIA) | 80 hrs/2-yr cycle with 24 govt hours |
| **Finding Elements** | 5 (Criteria, Condition, Cause, Consequence, Recommendation) | 4 (Criteria, Condition, Cause, Effect) |
| **Report Format** | Flexible | Specific format required |
| **Fraud Detection** | Expected to consider fraud risks | Explicit responsibility |
| **Quality** | QAIP (internal + external every 5 yrs) | QC standards (peer review every 3 yrs) |
| **Engagement Types** | Assurance + Consulting | Financial, Performance, Attestation |
| **Strategy Requirement** | Standard 9.1 explicit IA Strategy | §7.05-7.10 Planning |

### Government Internal Audit — Uses BOTH
Government internal audit functions often must meet both GIAS and GAGAS. Overlapping areas: independence, findings structure, QA, planning. GAGAS always more stringent where they overlap.

## 19. Common Charter Weaknesses (Identified in QAIP Assessments)
- Silent on consulting services scope
- Missing QAIP commitment
- No escalation protocol for disagreements
- Weak language on access ("reasonable" vs "unrestricted")
- Missing reference to GIAS (still cites 2017 IPPF)
- No reference to Three Lines Model
- CAE reporting line ambiguity
- Missing fraud response authority
- Not updated for public sector / GAGAS when applicable

## 20. Common Gap Areas Identified in QAIP Assessments

1. **QAIP documentation** — no formal QAIP plan (ironic)
2. **Strategic planning** — no written IA strategy (new GIAS 9.1 requirement)
3. **Independence** — CAE reporting line weaknesses
4. **Competency** — no skills matrix, no training plan
5. **Risk-based planning** — weak risk assessment methodology
6. **Engagement planning** — inadequate risk/control matrices
7. **Workpaper standards** — inconsistent documentation
8. **Supervision** — review evidence incomplete
9. **Reporting** — weak root cause analysis
10. **Monitoring** — action plan follow-up gaps
11. **Board reporting** — insufficient frequency/depth
12. **"Conforms with Standards" language** without external validation
